LAWS OF CRICKET — CONDENSED OFFICIAL MANUAL
(Unofficial study summary, structured like a governing-body rulebook)

PREAMBLE — THE SPIRIT OF CRICKET
Cricket owes much of its appeal to the fact that it should be played not only
within its Laws but also within the Spirit of the Game. Any action seen as
abusing this spirit causes injury to the game itself. The major responsibility
for ensuring fair play rests with the captains, but extends to all players,
match officials, and, in junior cricket, teachers, coaches and parents.

LAW 1 — THE PLAYERS
1.1 A match is played between two sides, each of eleven players, one of whom
shall be the captain.
1.2 If a captain is not available at any time, a deputy shall act for them.
1.3 A team may exceed eleven players only by prior agreement, but no more than
eleven may field at any time.
1.4 Nomination of players must be made to the umpires before the toss. A nominated
player may be replaced only with the consent of the opposing captain.

LAW 2 — THE UMPIRES
2.1 Before the toss, two umpires shall be appointed to control the game with
absolute impartiality as required by the Laws.
2.2 The umpires shall satisfy themselves before the match that the conduct of the
game will be strictly in accordance with the Laws.
2.3 An umpire may change a decision provided that such change is made promptly.
2.4 The umpires shall be the sole judges of fair and unfair play.
2.5 If an umpire is incapacitated, the players may agree on a replacement; if no
agreement is reached, the senior umpire shall make the appointment.

LAW 3 — THE SCORERS
3.1 Two scorers shall be appointed to record all runs scored, all wickets taken
and, where appropriate, the number of overs bowled.
3.2 The scorers shall accept and acknowledge all instructions and signals given to
them by the umpires.
3.3 The scorers shall frequently check the accuracy of their records with each
other and, at all reasonable intervals, with the umpires.

LAW 5 — THE BAT
5.1 The bat consists of two parts, a handle and a blade.
5.2 The overall length of the bat, when the lower portion of the handle is inserted,
shall not be more than 38 inches / 96.5 cm.
5.3 The blade of the bat shall not exceed 4.25 inches / 10.8 cm at the widest part,
5.4 The depth shall not exceed 2.64 inches / 6.7 cm, and the edges shall not exceed
1.56 inches / 4.0 cm. These limits were introduced to curb oversized bats.

LAW 6 — THE PITCH
6.1 The pitch is a rectangular area of the ground 22 yards / 20.12 m in length and
10 feet / 3.05 m in width.
6.2 The pitch shall not be watered during a match.
6.3 Before the toss and during the match, the umpires shall be the final judges of
the fitness of the pitch for play.

LAW 7 — THE CREASES
7.1 The bowling crease shall be the line through the middle of the stumps at each end.
7.2 The popping crease shall be in front of and parallel to the bowling crease, 4 feet
/ 1.22 m from it.
7.3 The return creases are the two lines at right angles to the bowling crease.

LAW 8 — THE WICKETS
8.1 The wicket consists of three wooden stumps with two bails on top.
8.2 The stumps shall be 28 inches / 71.1 cm above the playing surface.
8.3 The combined width of the three stumps shall be 9 inches / 22.86 cm.

LAW 18 — SCORING RUNS
18.1 A run is scored when the batters, at any time while the ball is in play, have
crossed and made good their ground from end to end.
18.2 A run is not scored if a batter is run out, or is out by any other method, or
if the batters fail to make good their ground.
18.3 If a boundary is scored, the umpire shall signal four runs if the ball touched
the ground before crossing the boundary, and six runs if it cleared the boundary
on the full.

LAW 19 — BOUNDARIES
19.1 Before the toss, the umpires shall agree the boundary of the field of play.
19.2 The allowance for a boundary shall be four runs if the ball reaches the boundary
after touching the ground, and six runs if it crosses the boundary on the full.

LAW 20 — DEAD BALL
20.1 The ball becomes dead when it is finally settled in the hands of the wicket-keeper
or the bowler; when a boundary is scored; when a batter is dismissed; or when it
lodges in the clothing of a batter or umpire.
20.2 The ball ceases to be dead when the bowler begins their run-up.

LAW 21 — NO BALL
21.1 A no ball is signalled if the bowler's front foot lands beyond the popping crease,
or if no part of the back foot lands within the return crease.
21.2 A no ball is also called for a throw (an illegal bowling action where the elbow
extends beyond 15 degrees).
21.3 The penalty for a no ball is one run, and the delivery does not count as one of
the over's six legal balls. A batter cannot be out bowled, caught, LBW or stumped
off a no ball.

LAW 22 — WIDE BALL
22.1 A wide is signalled if the ball passes wide of where the striker is standing and
also wide of the stumps, such that the batter cannot reasonably reach it with a
normal cricket stroke.
22.2 The penalty for a wide is one run, and the delivery does not count toward the over.

LAW 24 — LEG BYES AND BYES
24.1 Byes are runs scored from a legal delivery that the batter does not touch with bat
or body; the umpire signals a bye.
24.2 Leg byes are runs scored when the ball strikes the batter's body (not the bat) and
the batter was attempting a stroke or evading the ball.

LAW 32 — BOWLED
32.1 The striker is out bowled if their wicket is put down by a ball delivered by the
bowler, even if it first touches the bat or person.

LAW 33 — CAUGHT
33.1 The striker is out caught if a fielder catches the ball before it touches the ground,
after it has touched the striker's bat.

LAW 36 — LEG BEFORE WICKET (LBW)
36.1 The striker is out LBW if the ball would have hit the wicket, and it intercepted the
batter (not the bat) in line between wicket and wicket, provided the ball did not
pitch outside leg stump and the batter was not attempting to play a genuine stroke
when struck outside the line of off stump.

LAW 38 — RUN OUT
38.1 A batter is out run out if, while the ball is in play, the wicket is fairly put down
while the batter is out of their ground, except where the batter has already made
good their ground.

LAW 39 — STUMPED
39.1 The striker is out stumped if the wicket-keeper puts down the wicket while the striker
is out of their ground and not attempting a run, and the delivery was not a no ball.

LAW 41 — UNFAIR PLAY
41.1 The umpires shall intervene in cases of deliberate time-wasting, damaging the pitch,
dangerous or unfair bowling, and ball tampering.
41.2 Changing the condition of the ball other than by normal polishing on clothing,
drying it with a towel, or removing mud under supervision, is unfair.
